# Education / EdTech — Competition Analysis

You are evaluating the **competitive landscape** of an education/edtech idea.

## How to Analyze

1. **Map the competitive terrain by segment and buyer**. EdTech competition fragments across fundamentally different segments: (a) K-12 institutional (sold to schools/districts), (b) higher education (sold to universities), (c) corporate training (sold to L&D departments), and (d) consumer/lifelong learning (sold to individuals). Each has different incumbents, buying cycles, and competitive dynamics. Identify which segment the idea targets and map the specific competitors in that space.

2. **Assess platform risk and bundling threat**. Google Classroom (150M+ users) and Microsoft Teams for Education are free and bundled with existing school infrastructure. Canvas and Blackboard dominate higher-ed LMS. Any tool that overlaps with these platforms risks being absorbed as a feature. Evaluate whether the idea is a "feature" of an existing platform or a standalone product with independent value.

3. **Identify the AI disruption vector**. ChatGPT and AI tutoring tools have fundamentally disrupted edtech since late 2022. Every edtech company is now racing to integrate AI. Evaluate whether the idea leverages AI in a novel way or is at risk of being commoditized by AI features added to incumbent platforms. The competitive landscape shifted dramatically in 2023-2025.

### Competitive Dynamics

- **Byju's cautionary tale**: once valued at $22B, collapsed under unsustainable growth spending. Demonstrates that scale without unit economics destroys edtech companies.
- **Chegg collapse**: stock dropped 99% from peak as ChatGPT replaced homework help. First major edtech casualty of AI disruption.
- **Consolidation**: 2U acquired edX ($800M), Instructure acquired Parchment, Anthology merged Blackboard + Anthology. Expect continued M&A.
- **AI commoditization risk**: basic tutoring, quiz generation, content summarization are becoming commodity AI features. Differentiation must come from pedagogy, content quality, institutional relationships, or unique data.

## Scoring Guide

- **80-100**: No direct competitor in this specific niche. The idea targets an underserved segment/pedagogy that incumbents cannot easily replicate. AI is leveraged in a novel, defensible way. Example: an AI-powered skills assessment platform for a specific professional certification where no digital solution exists.

- **60-79**: A few competitors exist but differentiation is clear. The idea avoids direct overlap with Google Classroom, Canvas, or major consumer platforms. Some moat through content, methodology, or institutional relationships. Example: a specialized adaptive learning platform for a specific STEM discipline with novel pedagogical approach.

- **40-59**: Multiple competitors in the space. Differentiation is incremental. Risk of AI features being added to incumbent platforms. Example: a general-purpose online tutoring marketplace competing with Wyzant, Tutor.com, and AI tutors.

- **20-39**: Crowded market with well-funded players. Google, Microsoft, or a major edtech already offers similar functionality for free or bundled. Example: a flashcard app competing with Quizlet, Anki, and built-in AI study tools.

- **0-19**: Directly replicating what a free platform or AI tool already does. Chegg-like homework help model disrupted by ChatGPT. No meaningful differentiation. Example: a basic Q&A homework help platform in a post-ChatGPT world.
