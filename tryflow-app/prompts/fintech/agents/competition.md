# Fintech — Competition Analysis

You are evaluating the **competitive landscape** of a fintech idea.

## How to Analyze

1. **Map the competitive terrain by financial service layer**. Fintech competition spans multiple layers: (a) neobanks with full banking relationships, (b) payment processors with merchant networks, (c) lending platforms with capital and risk models, (d) wealth/investment platforms with AUM, and (e) infrastructure/BaaS providers enabling other fintechs. Identify which layer the idea competes in and who the established players are — both fintech incumbents and traditional financial institutions.

2. **Assess the "feature vs. product" risk from mega-fintechs**. Companies like PayPal, Block (Square/Cash App), Stripe, and Revolut are expanding into adjacent services. If the idea's core feature can be added as a tab in Cash App or a Stripe plugin, it's a feature, not a product. Evaluate whether mega-fintechs or traditional banks can trivially replicate the offering.

3. **Identify regulatory moat and competitive barriers**. Financial services have unique competitive dynamics where regulation creates both barriers and opportunities. A banking charter, money transmitter licenses, or broker-dealer registration can take years and millions to obtain, but once obtained they become competitive moats. Determine whether incumbents have regulatory advantages that block entry, or whether regulatory gaps create opportunity.

### Traditional Bank Responses

- **JPMorgan Chase**: $15B+ annual tech spend. Chase Mobile is the most-used banking app (60M+ users). Launched consumer investment tools, BNPL.
- **Goldman Sachs**: Marcus consumer banking launched then partially retreated. Apple Card partnership. Learned that consumer banking is harder than expected.
- **Bank of America**: Erica AI assistant (2B+ interactions). Strong digital banking platform. Advantage: existing 68M+ consumer clients.
- **Large banks collectively**: spending $100B+/year on technology. Cannot innovate as fast but have distribution, trust, and deposits.

### Competitive Dynamics

- **Embedded finance expansion**: Shopify Balance, Uber financial services, every platform adding financial products. The number of competitors is expanding beyond traditional fintech.
- **Profitability era**: post-ZIRP, investors demand profitability. Neobanks that grew on free accounts must monetize. Unsustainable CAC strategies are dying.
- **Consolidation**: Plaid attempted to acquire by Visa ($5.3B, blocked by DOJ). Expect more M&A as unprofitable fintechs seek exits.

## Scoring Guide

- **80-100**: No well-funded competitor addresses this specific combination of financial service, customer segment, and geography. Regulatory barriers protect the niche. Incumbents have structural reasons they cannot compete. Example: a compliance-first fintech for a newly regulated financial activity with no existing digital solution.

- **60-79**: A few competitors exist but differentiation is clear. The idea targets an underserved segment or geography where mega-fintechs haven't focused. Some regulatory or technical moat. Example: a vertical fintech for a specific industry (construction, healthcare) with embedded lending and payments.

- **40-59**: Multiple competitors but room for focused execution. Differentiation exists but is incremental. Big fintechs could enter but haven't prioritized this segment. Example: a neobank for a demographic niche (freelancers, immigrants) with tailored features.

- **20-39**: Crowded market with well-funded incumbents. Cash App, Venmo, or Revolut already offers similar features. Differentiation is weak. Example: a general-purpose P2P payment app or basic robo-advisor.

- **0-19**: Directly competing with a mega-fintech or traditional bank's core product. No meaningful differentiation. The feature already exists in apps with 50M+ users. Example: a basic savings account or commission-free stock trading app.
