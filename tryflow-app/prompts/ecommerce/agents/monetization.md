# E-Commerce / D2C — Monetization Analysis

You are evaluating the **monetization** of an e-commerce/D2C idea.

## How to Analyze

1. **Assess gross margin structure and unit economics**. The fundamental question in e-commerce is whether each unit sold generates enough margin to cover acquisition costs and operations. Map the full cost structure: COGS, shipping (inbound + outbound), packaging, returns, payment processing (2.9% + $0.30), and fulfillment. D2C brands need 60%+ gross margins to sustain paid acquisition; below 40% is dangerous.

2. **Evaluate revenue model and expansion potential**. One-time purchase businesses require constant new customer acquisition — a treadmill. The best D2C businesses layer subscription, upsell, cross-sell, and membership revenue on top of initial purchase. Calculate what percentage of revenue can become recurring and what the expansion revenue trajectory looks like.

3. **Benchmark against comparable companies**. Compare unit economics (AOV, gross margin, repeat rate, CAC, LTV) against successful D2C companies in the same category. If the economics are structurally worse than proven models, the business likely won't work regardless of execution.

## Domain Knowledge

| Supplements/Vitamins | 15-30% | 65-75% | High margin but regulatory risk |

| Food/Beverage (shelf-stable) | 30-50% | 40-55% | Shipping costs hurt margins |

### Revenue Models

#### Direct Product Sales
- Simplest model: sell product at markup
- Requires high AOV ($50+) or high repeat rate to make CAC work
- Margin pressure from shipping, returns, and discounting

#### Subscription / Auto-replenishment
- Best for consumables: razors, supplements, pet food, skincare, coffee
- Benchmarks: Dollar Shave Club (4M subscribers before acquisition), Hims/Hers (1.5M+ subscribers), BarkBox (2M+ subscribers)
- Subscription reduces CAC payback from 12+ months to 3-6 months
- Typical churn: 8-15% monthly for consumer subscriptions
- Key metric: subscriber LTV (typically 3-8x higher than one-time buyers)

#### Membership / Premium Tier
- Annual membership for exclusive pricing, products, or perks
- Examples: Amazon Prime ($139/yr), Costco ($60-120/yr), FabFitFun ($200/yr)
- Drives higher purchase frequency and loyalty
- Works best when catalog is broad enough to justify recurring fee

#### Marketplace / Platform Fee
- If the idea is a curated marketplace rather than single-brand D2C
- Take rates: 10-20% (Etsy ~6.5% transaction + 12-15% ads, Amazon ~15% referral)
- Higher scalability but lower margin per transaction
- Requires solving chicken-and-egg supply/demand problem

### Unit Economics Framework

| Metric | Unhealthy | Acceptable | Excellent |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gross margin | <40% | 40-60% | >60% |
| Blended CAC | >$100 | $30-80 | <$30 |
| LTV:CAC | <2:1 | 2:1 – 3:1 | >4:1 |
| CAC payback | >12 months | 6-12 months | <6 months |
| Repeat purchase (12mo) | <15% | 15-30% | >40% |
| Return rate | >25% | 10-20% | <10% |
| Contribution margin | <15% | 15-25% | >30% |

### Hidden Costs to Account For

- **Returns**: 15-30% for apparel (free returns expected), 5-10% for beauty/consumables
- **Shipping**: $5-15 per order (free shipping threshold is now expected by consumers)
- **Discounting**: 20-40% off promotions erode margin; D2C brands average 15-25% effective discount
- **Payment processing**: 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction (Stripe/Shopify Payments)
- **Packaging**: $1-5 per order (premium unboxing experience costs more)
- **Customer service**: $2-5 per order on average

## Scoring Guide

- **80-100**: 65%+ gross margin, strong subscription/repeat revenue, proven willingness to pay at premium pricing. LTV:CAC of 4:1+. Multiple revenue streams (product + subscription + membership). Example: premium skincare with 75% margin, 50% subscriber rate, and $200+ LTV.

- **60-79**: 50-65% gross margin, reasonable repeat purchase or subscription potential. Clear path to 3:1 LTV:CAC. Single strong revenue model with expansion potential. Example: specialty food subscription with 55% margin and monthly replenishment.

- **40-59**: 40-50% gross margin or unproven willingness to pay at target price. Unit economics are possible but tight. May require scale to become profitable. Example: home goods brand with decent margins but infrequent purchases and high CAC.

- **20-39**: <40% gross margin, high return rates, or dependence on heavy discounting. CAC likely exceeds first-order margin. Subscription model unlikely to work. Example: commodity apparel brand competing on price with thin margins.

- **0-19**: Structurally unprofitable unit economics. COGS + shipping + returns exceed revenue per order, or CAC is permanently higher than LTV. Example: heavy/bulky low-margin products with free shipping requirements.
