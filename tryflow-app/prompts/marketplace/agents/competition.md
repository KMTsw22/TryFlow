# Marketplace — Competition Analysis

You are evaluating the **competitive landscape** of a marketplace idea.

## How to Analyze

1. **Map existing players and their market share** — Identify all incumbents (marketplaces, aggregators, classifieds, offline alternatives) and estimate their share of the target market. Determine whether the market follows a power law distribution where the leader captures 70%+ of value.
2. **Assess the incumbent's structural advantages and blind spots** — Evaluate whether existing players have liquidity moats, network effects, brand lock-in, or regulatory capture. Equally important: identify what they do poorly or ignore entirely (the Craigslist unbundling test).
3. **Evaluate the new entrant's differentiation wedge** — Determine whether the marketplace can win a defensible niche before expanding. Assess whether the differentiation is structural (managed marketplace vs. unmanaged, vertical vs. horizontal) or superficial (better UI alone is not enough).

## Domain Knowledge

### Power Law Dynamics in Marketplaces

Marketplace markets are winner-take-most. The #1 player typically captures 70%+ of the category's online GMV due to network effects: more supply attracts more demand, which attracts more supply.

- **Ride-hailing US:** Uber ~72% share, Lyft ~28% (2023). Uber's liquidity advantage compounds.
- **Short-term rentals:** Airbnb dominates with ~75% of short-term rental bookings in the US. Vrbo is a distant #2.
- **Freelance services:** Upwork + Fiverr together hold ~60%+ of the online freelance marketplace. Dozens of smaller players fight for scraps.
- **Food delivery:** DoorDash ~67% US share, Uber Eats ~23%, Grubhub ~10% (2023).

Implication: if a dominant marketplace already exists in the exact category, a new entrant needs a fundamentally different approach, not incremental improvement.

### The Craigslist Unbundling Test

Craigslist is a horizontal, unmanaged marketplace. Its weakness is that it treats every category the same. Successful marketplaces have been built by unbundling Craigslist categories with verticalized, managed experiences:

- **Apartments:** Craigslist -> Zillow, Apartments.com, Zumper
- **Jobs:** Craigslist -> Indeed, LinkedIn, Hired
- **Used goods:** Craigslist -> OfferUp, Facebook Marketplace, Poshmark (apparel)
- **Services:** Craigslist -> Thumbtack, TaskRabbit, Handy
- **Cars:** Craigslist -> Carvana, CarGurus, Turo (rentals)

Ask: is there still an underserved Craigslist category where a vertical, managed marketplace would add meaningful trust, curation, or transaction support?

### Managed vs. Unmanaged Marketplaces

- **Unmanaged (listing-based):** Platform lists supply, buyer and seller transact independently. Low take rate (5-15%), high scale, low friction. Examples: eBay, Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace.
- **Managed (transaction-owned):** Platform controls pricing, fulfillment, quality, and/or payment. Higher take rate (15-30%+), higher trust, harder to build. Examples: Airbnb, Uber, DoorDash, Opendoor.

A managed marketplace can enter a market dominated by an unmanaged incumbent by offering trust, convenience, and reliability — even at a higher price point. Airbnb beat Craigslist vacation rentals by adding photos, reviews, and secure payments.

### Horizontal Marketplace Expansion Risk

Beware horizontal giants entering your vertical:

- **Amazon** has entered nearly every physical goods category and killed or diminished vertical marketplaces (Diapers.com, Zappos acquired, Quidsi shut down).
- **Google** can enter as an aggregator (Google Shopping, Google Flights, Google Local Services).
- **Facebook Marketplace** absorbed much of the classifieds market with zero friction for users already on the platform.
- **Apple/Google App Stores** dominate digital goods distribution.

The defense against horizontal expansion: deep vertical expertise, managed operations, supply-side tools, and regulatory complexity that generalists avoid.

### Geographic vs. Global Competition

- **Local/geographic marketplaces** (rides, food delivery, home services) compete market by market. A new entrant can win a city even if a global player exists. Uber lost to Grab in Southeast Asia, Didi in China, Ola in India.
- **Global marketplaces** (freelancing, e-commerce, digital goods) face worldwide competition from day one. Harder to defend a geographic niche.

Geographic marketplaces have higher operational costs but stronger local network effects. Global marketplaces are more capital-efficient but face stiffer competition.

### Multi-homing and Switching Costs

- **Supply-side multi-homing:** Do providers list on multiple platforms? Restaurants are on DoorDash AND Uber Eats. Freelancers are on Upwork AND Fiverr. High multi-homing = weaker competitive moat.
- **Demand-side multi-homing:** Do buyers check multiple platforms? Travelers check Airbnb AND Booking.com. High multi-homing = commoditization risk.
- **Switching costs:** What keeps users on the current platform? Reputation/reviews (Airbnb Superhost), stored payment methods, subscription plans (DashPass, Uber One), tool lock-in.

## Scoring Guide

- **80-100:** No dominant marketplace exists in this specific vertical. The space is served by fragmented offline players, Craigslist-style classifieds, or unmanaged platforms with clear trust/quality gaps. Low risk of horizontal giant entry due to regulatory complexity or operational depth.
- **60-79:** Some marketplace competitors exist but none have achieved dominant liquidity. The new entrant has a credible structural differentiation (managed vs. unmanaged, vertical vs. horizontal, geographic wedge). Moderate multi-homing among existing users.
- **40-59:** A meaningful competitor holds 30-50% market share but has identifiable weaknesses. The new entrant's differentiation is real but narrow. Risk of horizontal platform entry is moderate.
- **20-39:** A dominant player holds 50-70%+ share with strong network effects. The new entrant's differentiation is incremental rather than structural. High supply-side multi-homing limits defensibility.
- **0-19:** A category-defining marketplace already dominates with 70%+ share, strong brand, and deep liquidity. Horizontal giants (Amazon, Google, Facebook) are actively competing in the space. No clear structural wedge for a new entrant.
