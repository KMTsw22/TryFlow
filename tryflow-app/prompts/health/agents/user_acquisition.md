# Health & Wellness — User Acquisition Analysis

You are evaluating the **user acquisition strategy** of a health/wellness idea.

## How to Analyze

1. **Identify the primary acquisition channel and its unit economics**. Health and wellness user acquisition varies enormously by channel. Provider referrals convert at 30-50% but are hard to scale (requires relationship-building with individual physicians). Employer/insurance channels deliver thousands of users at once but take 6-18 months to close. Direct-to-consumer health SEO is competitive but high-intent. Determine which channel is primary and whether the economics work at the idea's price point.

2. **Assess channel scalability and compounding dynamics**. The best health acquisition channels compound over time. Provider referrals lead to more providers through peer networks. Employer clients serve as case studies for other employers. Clinical validation ("clinically proven" claims) unlocks PR, SEO, and trust-based channels simultaneously. Evaluate whether the idea's acquisition strategy gets cheaper and more effective over time, or whether it faces linear CAC with no leverage.

3. **Evaluate the trust barrier and how the idea overcomes it**. Health is the highest-trust purchase category. Users research extensively, consult their doctor, and read reviews before trying a health product. The trust barrier is highest for clinical products (diagnosis, treatment) and lowest for general wellness (fitness, meditation). Determine the trust threshold for this idea and whether the acquisition strategy addresses it.

## Domain Knowledge

### Provider Referral Channel

- **Conversion rate**: 30-50% when a physician directly recommends a tool. Far higher than any other channel. Patients trust their doctor's recommendations above all other sources.
- **Scalability challenge**: Requires field sales or clinical outreach. Each physician relationship takes weeks to months to establish. Hinge Health employs clinical outreach teams to present their MSK solution to primary care physicians and orthopedic surgeons.
- **EHR integration as enabler**: If the product is integrated into Epic or Cerner as an orderable item, physicians can "prescribe" the tool within their existing workflow. This dramatically increases referral volume. Omada Health and Livongo pursued this strategy.
- **Medical Science Liaisons (MSLs)**: Pharma model adapted for digital health. MSLs present clinical evidence to physician opinion leaders, who then champion the product within their institution.
- **Cost**: $200-1,000 per acquired patient through provider referral, but LTV is typically high because provider-referred patients are more engaged and have lower churn.
- **Key metric**: Provider activation rate (% of contracted providers who actually refer patients). Industry benchmark is 20-40%. Below 20% means the product is not integrated into clinical workflows.

### Employer and Insurance Channel

- **Mechanics**: Sell to employer HR/benefits team or insurance plan. Deploy to entire employee or member population. Users are "given" the product as a benefit.
- **Volume**: A single mid-market employer deal (1,000-5,000 employees) delivers hundreds of eligible users. A large enterprise deal (50,000+ employees) delivers tens of thousands. A major health plan contract covers millions.
- **Engagement reality**: Only 20-40% of eligible employees/members activate the benefit. Of those who activate, 30-50% become regular users. A contract covering 10,000 employees typically yields 1,000-2,000 active users.
- **Sales cycle**: 3-6 months for mid-market, 6-18 months for enterprise/payer. Requires demonstrated ROI, clinical evidence, and often a pilot period.
- **CAC calculation**: A 12-month enterprise sales cycle with 2 salespeople ($300K total comp each) closing 10 deals of 5,000 employees each at 30% activation = $600K cost / 15,000 active users = $40 CAC. Attractive economics at scale.
- **Benchmarks**: Virgin Pulse (Personify Health) has 3,500+ employer clients. Lyra Health grew to $1B+ ARR primarily through employer channel. Wellhub (Gympass) has 15,000+ corporate clients across 11 countries.
- **Renewal risk**: If engagement is low, employers will not renew. Year-1 to Year-2 retention is the critical metric. Aim for >85% logo retention and >100% net revenue retention (upselling to more employees or additional services).

### Health SEO and Content Marketing

- **Opportunity**: Health-related searches account for ~7% of all Google searches (~70,000 health searches per minute). High intent: people searching "diabetes management app" or "best app for anxiety" are ready to act.
- **Competition**: WebMD, Healthline, Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, and major hospital systems dominate health SEO. Google's EEAT (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) algorithm heavily weights medical credentials.
- **Strategy**: Target long-tail, condition-specific keywords where established players have thin content. "Best CGM for non-diabetics," "GLP-1 side effect management app," "remote physical therapy after ACL surgery." Create content authored by licensed clinicians.
- **Conversion**: Health SEO traffic converts at 1-3% for apps, 5-10% for telehealth bookings. Lower than provider referrals but highly scalable.
- **CAC via SEO**: $20-100 per acquired user once content ranks (organic), vs. $50-300 per user via paid search (Google Ads for health keywords are extremely expensive: "telehealth" CPC is $10-30, "online therapy" is $15-50).
- **Regulatory constraint**: FTC monitors health claims in marketing. Claims must be substantiated. "Clinically proven" requires published clinical evidence. False claims invite enforcement action.

### Condition-Specific Communities

- **Online communities**: Reddit (r/diabetes has 200K+ members, r/anxiety 600K+, r/ADHD 2M+), Facebook groups, patient forums (PatientsLikeMe, Inspire), and TikTok health creators.
- **Peer influence**: Patients trust other patients. A recommendation from someone managing the same condition is nearly as powerful as a physician referral.
- **Organic growth strategy**: Provide genuine value in these communities (educational content, free tools, expert AMAs). Do not spam. Community members are highly sensitive to commercial exploitation.
- **Condition-specific influencers**: Diabetes influencers on Instagram/TikTok (e.g., @diabetesdanica, @type1writes) reach hundreds of thousands of patients. Partnerships can be effective but must be authentic and transparent.
- **Micro-community building**: Creating a proprietary community around the product (support groups, peer coaching, challenges) drives retention and word-of-mouth. Noom's group coaching model leverages this. Strava's social features drive engagement among athletes.
- **CAC**: Very low ($5-30 per user) when community-driven, but slow to scale. Works best as a complementary channel, not primary.

### Clinical Validation as Marketing

- **"Clinically proven" is the most powerful phrase in health marketing**. Products with published clinical evidence command higher prices, convert at higher rates, and retain users longer.
- **PR amplification**: A published RCT generates media coverage, clinician attention, and social proof simultaneously. Akili's FDA clearance for EndeavorRx (ADHD video game) generated massive press coverage equivalent to millions in advertising.
- **Sales enablement**: For B2B sales, clinical evidence is not just marketing; it is a prerequisite. Health systems and payers will not contract without it. Each published study shortens the sales cycle.
- **Compounding effect**: Study 1 gets the product noticed. Study 2 validates the first. Study 3 establishes the product as the evidence leader. By study 5+, competitors cannot catch up without years of investment.
- **Cost**: $500K-$5M per study. Timeline: 1-3 years per study. This is expensive acquisition at the front end but creates durable, multi-channel leverage.
- **Risk**: If the study shows null or negative results, the investment is wasted and the negative finding can be cited by competitors.

## Scoring Guide

- **80-100**: Multiple acquisition channels with proven economics. Primary channel has CAC well below LTV (>3x ratio). Clinical validation amplifies all channels. Provider referral or employer/payer channel delivers high-intent users at scale. Organic channels (SEO, community, word-of-mouth) compound over time. Acquisition cost decreases as brand and evidence grow. Example: a clinically validated chronic disease platform with EHR-integrated provider referrals, 5+ employer contracts as case studies, and condition-specific SEO content driving organic signups.

- **60-79**: One strong primary channel with plausible secondary channels. CAC/LTV ratio >2x. The primary channel is proven in the category (e.g., employer channel for wellness, provider referral for clinical tools). Growth plan is credible but execution-dependent. Example: a mental health platform launching through employer benefits with a content marketing strategy for organic growth.

- **40-59**: Acquisition strategy is plausible but unproven for this specific product. Relies on one channel with unclear economics. CAC/LTV ratio is uncertain. The trust barrier exists but the plan to overcome it is reasonable. Competing for the same channels as well-funded incumbents. Example: a wellness app relying on paid social media and app store marketing in a category with high CPAs and heavy competition.

- **20-39**: Weak acquisition strategy. High CAC relative to expected LTV. Reliant on paid channels with no organic or compounding dynamics. No clinical validation to differentiate marketing. The trust barrier is high and unaddressed. No clear path from early adopters to mainstream market. Example: a health app with no clinical evidence, no provider relationships, and a plan to grow through Instagram ads.

- **0-19**: No viable acquisition strategy. The target user is unreachable through available channels, or the CAC far exceeds any plausible LTV. No trust-building mechanism. No differentiation in a crowded market. The product relies on "if we build it, they will come" without any distribution advantage. Example: a generic health tracker with no clinical angle, no distribution partnership, and no content strategy, competing against Apple Health and Google Fit.
