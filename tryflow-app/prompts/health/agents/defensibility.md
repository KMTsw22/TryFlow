# Health & Wellness — Defensibility Analysis

You are evaluating the **defensibility** of a health/wellness idea.

## How to Analyze

1. **Identify which moat types the idea can realistically build**. Healthcare offers some of the strongest possible competitive moats, but they take years and significant capital to establish. Clinical evidence is expensive to replicate ($500K-$50M for RCTs). FDA clearance creates a 1-2 year regulatory moat. Provider networks require individual contracting with clinicians. Payer contracts take years to negotiate. Assess which moats are available to this idea and how long they take to build.

2. **Evaluate the strength and durability of each moat**. Not all moats are equal. FDA clearance is a hard barrier but competitors will eventually clear it too. Clinical evidence compounds over time: each new study adds to the body of evidence, and replicating 5 years of published outcomes data is nearly impossible. Health data accumulation creates a flywheel where more data improves algorithms, which improves outcomes, which attracts more users and data. Rank the moats by strength and timeline.

3. **Assess trust as a strategic asset**. Healthcare is the highest-trust category in business. Patients trust their doctor, their hospital, and established brands. Building trust in a new health brand takes years of consistent, safe, validated performance. A single safety incident (data breach, misdiagnosis, adverse event) can destroy a health brand permanently. Evaluate whether the idea can build and maintain trust, and whether that trust becomes a competitive advantage.

## Domain Knowledge

### Clinical Evidence as a Moat

- **Cost to replicate**: A well-designed RCT in digital health costs $500K-$5M. Pear Therapeutics spent ~$50M across multiple RCTs for its digital therapeutics pipeline. Most startups cannot afford this, creating a barrier for those that can.
- **Time to replicate**: Designing, conducting, and publishing an RCT takes 2-4 years. By the time a competitor replicates your evidence, you have 2-4 more years of real-world data.
- **Compounding advantage**: Omada Health has published 30+ peer-reviewed studies over 10+ years. A new competitor would need years to build a comparable evidence base, and payers weigh the totality of evidence.
- **Real-world evidence (RWE)**: Outcomes data from deployed products is increasingly valued by payers and regulators. Virta Health publishes 5-year diabetes reversal data. This longitudinal evidence is impossible to fast-track.
- **Limitation**: Clinical evidence is a moat only if the buyer (payer, employer, health system) requires it. Consumer wellness buyers rarely ask for peer-reviewed studies.

### FDA Clearance as a Moat

- **Timeline**: 510(k) clearance takes 6-18 months. De Novo takes 12-24 months. PMA takes 2-5 years.
- **Cost**: $100K-$500K for 510(k), $500K-$2M for De Novo, $5M-$50M for PMA including clinical trials.
- **Competitive advantage duration**: FDA clearance provides a 1-2 year head start. Competitors can file their own 510(k) citing yours as a predicate, potentially clearing in 6-12 months.
- **Stronger version**: If your product defines a new device category via De Novo, you set the regulatory standard. Competitors must meet the criteria you helped establish. IDx-DR (autonomous diabetic retinopathy screening) created a De Novo classification that subsequent entrants must match.
- **Predicate leverage**: Once cleared, your product becomes a predicate for others, but it also means your clearance is validated and established in the market.

### Provider Network Effects

- **Contracted clinician networks**: Telehealth companies like Teladoc and BetterHelp have networks of thousands of licensed clinicians. Building this requires individual credentialing, contracting, state licensing, and quality monitoring.
- **Referral relationships**: Provider-to-provider referrals are the highest-conversion channel in healthcare. A specialist network that receives referrals from PCPs has a powerful flywheel. Hinge Health's MSK program gets referrals from employer health plans and PCPs.
- **Switching costs**: Clinicians who learn a clinical workflow tool resist switching. Epic's dominance is partly because clinicians trained on Epic do not want to learn a new EHR. Smaller clinical tools benefit from the same dynamic.
- **Supply constraint**: There is a genuine shortage of healthcare providers (estimated 124,000 physician shortage in the US by 2034, per AAMC). Locking in provider supply is a strategic asset. BetterHelp and Talkspace compete fiercely for therapist supply.

### Health Data Accumulation

- **Data flywheel**: More users generate more health data, which trains better algorithms, which produce better outcomes, which attract more users. Tempus (oncology data) has genomic and clinical data on 500,000+ cancer patients, making its AI models increasingly difficult to replicate.
- **Longitudinal data**: Years of continuous patient data (wearable readings, lab results, medication history, outcomes) is uniquely valuable and impossible to recreate retroactively. Flatiron Health's longitudinal oncology data was worth $1.9B to Roche.
- **Data network effects**: Each new data source (EHR, wearable, claims) makes the dataset more valuable than the sum of its parts. Cross-referencing CGM data with sleep data, medication adherence, and clinical outcomes creates insights no single data source can provide.
- **Privacy constraints as moat**: Paradoxically, health data privacy regulations (HIPAA, GDPR) make it harder for new entrants to accumulate data. Incumbents with existing BAAs, consent frameworks, and de-identification pipelines have structural advantages.

### Payer Contracts

- **Sales cycle**: Negotiating a contract with a major health insurer (UnitedHealth, Anthem, Aetna, Humana, BCBS) takes 12-36 months from first meeting to signed contract.
- **Evidence requirements**: Payers require clinical evidence, actuarial analysis of cost savings, and often a pilot with outcomes data. This alone takes 1-2 years.
- **Contract duration**: Payer contracts are typically 2-3 years with annual renewals. Switching costs are high because integration with claims systems, member enrollment workflows, and clinical pathways is complex.
- **Network of contracts**: A company with 5 major payer contracts covering 100M+ lives has a moat that would take a competitor 3-5 years to replicate even with a better product.
- **Benchmarks**: Livongo had contracts with multiple large employers and payers before its Teladoc acquisition. Omada Health spent years building payer relationships that now cover millions of lives.

### Trust as a Competitive Advantage

- **Trust premium**: Patients will pay more and be more compliant with tools from trusted brands. Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, and Johns Hopkins can charge premium prices partly on brand trust.
- **Trust takes years to build**: Consistent safety record, positive clinical outcomes, endorsements from respected clinicians, and absence of data breaches build trust gradually.
- **Trust is destroyed instantly**: A single data breach (Anthem 2015: 80M records), a product safety issue (Theranos fraud), or a clinical harm event can permanently damage a health brand.
- **Institutional trust transfer**: Partnerships with trusted institutions (Mayo Clinic, academic medical centers, well-known physicians) can accelerate trust-building. Noom and Calm both leverage clinical advisory boards for credibility.
- **Outcomes data as sales collateral**: Published outcomes data ("our patients achieved 1.2% A1c reduction") is the most powerful sales tool in healthcare. It simultaneously proves efficacy and builds trust. Each year of published outcomes makes the sales conversation easier and competitors' conversations harder.

## Scoring Guide

- **80-100**: Multiple reinforcing moats that take 3+ years to replicate. FDA clearance (De Novo or PMA) combined with extensive clinical evidence (5+ published studies). Established payer contracts covering significant lives. Proprietary health data flywheel with longitudinal data. Strong institutional trust and brand. Example: an FDA-cleared digital therapeutic with 5+ RCTs, contracts with 3+ major payers, and 5 years of real-world outcomes data.

- **60-79**: Two or more meaningful moats in development. FDA 510(k) clearance or equivalent regulatory approval. Growing clinical evidence base (2-3 studies). Initial payer or health system contracts. Data accumulation underway with clear flywheel dynamics. Trust building through clinical partnerships. Example: an AI diagnostic tool with FDA clearance and contracts with 10+ health systems, actively accumulating proprietary training data.

- **40-59**: One clear moat with additional moats plausible but unproven. Clinical evidence in early stages (1 study or pilot data). Health data accumulating but not yet at a defensible scale. Provider network or payer relationships in early development. The product has some switching costs but they are not yet strong. Example: a chronic disease platform with one published pilot study and a growing provider network in a specific geography.

- **20-39**: Weak defensibility. No regulatory moat (general wellness, no FDA involvement). No clinical evidence requirement from buyers. Data accumulation is undifferentiated (same data available to competitors). Low switching costs. Trust has not been established. The idea could be replicated by a well-resourced competitor in 6-12 months. Example: a wellness app with a unique UX but no clinical validation, no proprietary data, and no payer relationships.

- **0-19**: No defensibility. The product is a feature, not a company. No regulatory barriers. No clinical evidence. No proprietary data. No switching costs. No trust advantage. A big tech platform could replicate this with an iOS or Android update. Example: a hydration reminder app or basic symptom checker with no clinical integration.
